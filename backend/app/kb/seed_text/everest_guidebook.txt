Golden  rule  of  documentation If  you  do  not  document  it,  then  technically  it  didn’t  happen.  Before  writing  any  call  note,  ask  yourself:  would  I  be  comfortable  reading  this  aloud  in  the  office  —  or  in  a  courtroom?  If  not,  revise  it.

2.1  Mandatory  disclosures  
Call  Recording  Disclosure  (CRD)  —  open  every  call “This  is  [your  name],  all  calls  are  recorded  … ” 
Mini-Miranda  /  Debt  Collector  Disclosure  (DCD) “This  is  a  communication  from  a  debt  collector.  This  is  an  attempt  to  collect  a  debt,  and  any  information  obtained  will  be  used  for  that  purpose.” 
ALL  non-location  messages  and  talk-offs  must  include  the  full  Mini-Miranda  disclosure. The  CRD  is  given  the  
moment
 
you

to

with
 
the

Mini-Miranda/DCD
 
is

2.2  Authentication  before  substance

You  may  not  discuss  account  specifics  until  the  right  party  is  authenticated.  Authenticate  by  one  of  the  
following:
 
▪  Date  of  Birth  (DOB),  ▪  Social  Security  Number  (SSN)  —  typically  the  last  four,  ▪  Full  mailing  address  (use  as  a  last  resort).  
A  mumble  is  not  a  “yes”  —  confirm  affirmative  consent Never  accept  an  ambiguous  “uh-huh”  or  “mhm”  as  verification  or  as  agreement  to  a  payment.  If  the  consumer’s  answer  is  unclear,  ask  for  an  explicit  confirmation:  “I’m  sorry,  I  didn’t  hear  you  clearly  —  is  that  a  yes?”  A  clean,  recorded  “yes”  protects  both  the  consumer  and  the  company. 
The  three-step  method  for  reluctant  verification  When  a  consumer  hesitates  to  verify,  don’t  surrender  the  call  immediately.  Escalate  gently  through  three  
steps:
 
1.  Direct  ask:  “Can  you  please  verify  your  date  of  birth  for  security?”  2.  Remind  &  re-ask:  “I  already  have  your  DOB  in  front  of  me;  I  just  need  you  to  confirm  it  so  I  know  I’m  
speaking
 
to
 
the
 
correct
 
person.”
 3.  Provide  partial  info:  “If  it  makes  it  easier,  I  have  June  1985  —  is  that  correct?”  (give  month/year  only).  Alternate  option:  ask  them  to  verify  the  last  four  (or  full)  digits  of  the  original-creditor  account  number.  
The  stalemate  —  terminate  without  disclosing If  they  still  refuse,  end  the  call  without  disclosing  the  debt:  “I  cannot  proceed  as  I’m  unable  to  verify  your  identity.  I  wouldn’t  want  to  discuss  private  financial  matters  with  the  wrong  person.”  Then  handle  as  an  RTA  (below). 
If  the  consumer  refuses  to  authenticate Do  not  disclose  account  details.  If  they  identify  by  name  but  refuse  DOB/SSN/address,  the  account  is  handled  as  an  RTA  (Refuse  To  Authenticate)  —  see  the  RTA  and  Statusing  sections.

3.  The  11-Point  Talk-Off:  Structure  &  Purpose  
Every  collection  call  follows  the  same  11-point  structure.  The  first  half  is  the  Opening;  the  second  half  is  the  
Negotiation.
 
Knowing
 
the
 
structure
 
lets
 
you
 
stay
 
in
 
control,
 
cover
 
every
 
compliance
 
point,
 
and
 
move
 
naturally
 
toward
 
resolution.
 
# Talk-Off  Point Phase Purpose 
1 Setup Opening 
Identify  yourself,  give  CRD,  reach  &  authenticate  the  consumer. 
2 Transition Opening Give  Mini-Miranda/DCD,  client  update,  explain  account  info. 
3 Listen  &  Answer Opening Hear  concerns,  rebut,  answer  questions,  address  objections. 
4 Pros  of  Payment Opening Sell  the  benefits  of  resolving  the  account. 
5 Request  Intentions Opening 
Gain  commitment:  “this  is  something  you’d  like  to  resolve,  right?” 
6 3  BIF  Questions Negotiation Ask  all  three;  push  conversationally  for  the  3  indicators. 
7 Qualify  /  A-Page Negotiation Gather  financial  picture  to  find  alternative  arrangements. 
8 Hold  for  Approval Negotiation Check  with  supervisor  /  2nd  voice;  build  value  of  the  hold. 
9 Stress  Urgency Negotiation Short  timeframes,  client  update,  banking  commitment. 
10 Provide  Offers Negotiation SIF  pyramid:  BIF  /  BIF  in  parts  /  SIF  —  offer  high,  leave  room. 
11 OTB  Resources Negotiation Return  to  probing  for  out-to-borrow  funds  to  close. 
3.1  The  3  Indicators  (buying  signs  &  commitment)  Throughout  the  BIF  questions,  push  conversationally  for  the  three  indicators  of  commitment.  These  tell  you  
what
 
the
 
consumer
 
is
 
really
 
offering
 
and
 
where
 
the
 
deal
 
will
 
close:
 
1.   Date  of  1st  Payment 2.   Banking  Commitment  (ACH  /  Credit  /  Debit) 3.   Number  of  Payments

7.1  Pushing  for  answers  at  the  BIF  questions  Move  down  this  ladder  conversationally  —  each  rung  narrows  toward  a  real  number  and  a  real  timeframe:

7.2  Negotiation  guidelines  ▪  Conversationally  push  for  the  3  indicators  during  the  BIF  questions.  ▪  Work  the  numbers  —  understand  the  buying  signs  and  what  the  consumer  is  actually  offering.  ▪  Leave  yourself  room  to  negotiate  and  gain  commitment.  ▪  If  they  won’t  commit  to  the  3  indicators,  set  the  initial  offer  at  2×  where  you  expect  to  close. ▪  When  the  consumer  doesn’t  take  the  initial  offer,  use  a  counteroffer.  
Questions  to  gain  commitment  before  a  secondary  offer  ▪  How  close  are  you  to  the  current  offer(s)?  →  (#  of  pays)  ▪  If  I  got  that  approved,  you’re  willing  to  set  it  up?  →  (banking  info)  ▪  First  payment  available  today?  →  (date  of  1st  pay)  ▪  Will  you  be  using  ACH/CC  to  secure  the  payments?  →  (banking  info)  
Using  a  2nd  voice  correctly If  you  HAVE  rapport  and  buying  signs  →  give  the  initial  offer  yourself. If  you  do  NOT  have  rapport  and/or  buying  signs  →  pass  to  a  2nd  voice.  A  2nd  voice  can  push  for  resolution  only  when  used  correctly. 
Bring  a  manager  or  closer  (the  turnover,  or  “T.O.”)  onto  the  line  when  the  negotiation  stalls.  Typical  triggers:  
▪  The  consumer  demands  a  settlement  below  the  approved  floor.  ▪  The  consumer  wants  a  very  low  monthly  payment  that  won’t  resolve  the  file  in  a  reasonable  timeframe.  ▪  The  consumer  clearly  has  the  means  but  refuses  to  pay.  ▪  The  consumer  becomes  hostile  and  you’ve  lost  control  of  the  call.
